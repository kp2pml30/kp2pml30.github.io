Four Ways to Say "Seems Like"

Japanese has four main constructions for expressing appearance, conjecture, and hearsay. They look similar but differ in the source of information and how they attach to words.

Overview

FormSource of informationNuance
～そうだ (appearance)Visual impression, gut feeling"It looks like it will..." — judging by how something looks right now.
～そうだ (hearsay)Someone told you, you read/heard it"I heard that..." — reporting secondhand information.
～ようだ / ～みたいだEvidence, reasoning, your own senses"It seems like..." — a conclusion based on what you observe or deduce.
～らしいIndirect evidence, general reputation"Apparently..." — based on what you've gathered from various sources.

1. ～そうだ — Appearance

Based on how something looks. You see it and judge. Attaches to the stem (before い/だ).

Formation

い-adjectivedrop い + そうおいしい → おいしそう — looks delicious
な-adjectivestem + そう元気げんき→ 元気そう — looks healthy
いい (irregular)よさそうよさそう — looks good
ない (negative)なさそうおいしくなさそう — doesn't look delicious

～そう behaves as a な-adjective:

2. ～そうだ — Hearsay

Reporting what you heard or read. Attaches to the plain form (dictionary/た/ない/etc.) directly.

Formation

Word typeRuleExample
Verb (present)plain form + そうだ雨が降るあめがふるそうだ — I heard it will rain
Verb (past)た form + そうだ雨が降ったふったそうだ — I heard it rained
Verb (negative)ない form + そうだ雨が降らないふらないそうだ — I heard it won't rain
い-adjectiveplain form + そうだおいしいそうだ — I heard it's delicious
な-adjectivestem + だそうだ静かしずかだそうだ — I heard it's quiet
Nounnoun + だそうだ学生がくせいだそうだ — I heard they're a student

Key difference from appearance そう: hearsay attaches to the complete plain form (おいしいそう), appearance attaches to the stem (おいし∅そう).

3. ～ようだ / ～みたいだ — Conjecture from Evidence

A conclusion you draw from evidence or your own senses. ～みたいだ is the casual equivalent of ～ようだ.

4. ～らしい — Apparently

Based on indirect evidence — things you've heard, read, or gathered. Also used for "typical of" (～らしい as a quality).

Formation

Word typeRuleExample
Verbplain form + らしい雨が降るらしい — apparently it will rain
い-adjectiveplain form + らしいおいしいらしい — apparently it's delicious
な-adjectivestem + らしい静からしい — apparently it's quiet
Nounnoun + らしい学生らしい — apparently they're a student

Conjugation

～らしい conjugates as an い-adjective:

FormExample
Plain降るらしい
Polite降るらしいです
Negative降るらしくない
Past降るらしかった

～らしい as "typical of"

When attached directly to a noun, ～らしい can also mean "typical of" or "befitting":

  • 男らしいおとこらしい— manly, masculine
  • 子供らしいこどもらしい— childlike (in a natural/positive way)
  • 春らしいはるらしい— spring-like
  • 学生らしいがくせいらしい— befitting a student
