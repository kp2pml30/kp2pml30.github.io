Four Ways to Say "Seems Like"

Japanese has four main constructions for expressing appearance, conjecture, and hearsay. They look similar but differ in the source of information and how they attach to words.

Overview

Form Source of information Nuance
～そうだ (appearance) Visual impression, gut feeling "It looks like it will..." — judging by how something looks right now.
～そうだ (hearsay) Someone told you, you read/heard it "I heard that..." — reporting secondhand information.
～ようだ / ～みたいだ Evidence, reasoning, your own senses "It seems like..." — a conclusion based on what you observe or deduce.
～らしい Indirect evidence, general reputation "Apparently..." — based on what you've gathered from various sources.

1. ～そうだ — Appearance

Based on how something looks. You see it and judge. Attaches to the stem (before い/だ).

Formation

Word type Rule Example
Verb ます stem + そう 降ります（ふります）→ 降りそう — looks like it will rain
い-adjective drop い + そう おいしい → おいしそう — looks delicious
な-adjective stem + そう 元気（げんき）→ 元気そう — looks healthy
いい (irregular) よさそう よさそう — looks good
ない (negative) なさそう おいしくなさそう — doesn't look delicious

Conjugation of ～そう

～そう behaves as a な-adjective:

Form Example (おいしそう)
Plainおいしそうだ
Politeおいしそうです
Negativeおいしそうじゃない
Pastおいしそうだった
Noun modificationおいしそうな料理（りょうり）
Adverbialおいしそうに食べる（たべる）

2. ～そうだ — Hearsay

Reporting what you heard or read. Attaches to the plain form (dictionary/た/ない/etc.) directly.

Formation

Word type Rule Example
Verb (present) plain form + そうだ 雨が降る（あめがふる）そうだ — I heard it will rain
Verb (past) た form + そうだ 雨が降った（ふった）そうだ — I heard it rained
Verb (negative) ない form + そうだ 雨が降らない（ふらない）そうだ — I heard it won't rain
い-adjective plain form + そうだ おいしいそうだ — I heard it's delicious
な-adjective stem + だそうだ 静か（しずか）だそうだ — I heard it's quiet
Noun noun + だそうだ 学生（がくせい）だそうだ — I heard they're a student

Key difference from appearance そう: hearsay attaches to the complete plain form (おいしいそう), appearance attaches to the stem (おいし∅そう).

3. ～ようだ / ～みたいだ — Conjecture from Evidence

A conclusion you draw from evidence or your own senses. ～みたいだ is the casual equivalent of ～ようだ.

静かみたいだ
Noun noun + の + ようだ

4. ～らしい — Apparently

Based on indirect evidence — things you've heard, read, or gathered. Also used for "typical of" (～らしい as a quality).

Formation

Word type Rule Example
Verb plain form + らしい 雨が降るらしい — apparently it will rain
い-adjective plain form + らしい おいしいらしい — apparently it's delicious
な-adjective stem + らしい 静からしい — apparently it's quiet
Noun noun + らしい 学生らしい — apparently they're a student

Conjugation

～らしい conjugates as an い-adjective:

Form Example
Plain降るらしい
Polite降るらしいです
Negative降るらしくない
Past降るらしかった

～らしい as "typical of"

When attached directly to a noun, ～らしい can also mean "typical of" or "befitting":

  • 男らしい（おとこらしい）— manly, masculine
  • 子供らしい（こどもらしい）— childlike (in a natural/positive way)
  • 春らしい（はるらしい）— spring-like
  • 学生らしい（がくせいらしい）— befitting a student
